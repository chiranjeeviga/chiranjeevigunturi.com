PayLah!, DBS Singapore, 2014

The problem

PayLah was about to launch for the first time, and a viral loop hadn’t been scoped into that first release. The business landed on a $5/$5 referral idea close to launch.

How would you create a user referral feature that moves real money across so many downstream systems, and still works for someone who hasn’t even downloaded PayLah yet?

What I did

  • Mapped the full user journey for both sender and receiver, down to the SMS each one got.
  • The call that mattered: designed the system to need minimal changes from the downstream teams.
  • Rallied several teams to build their parts and ship in days.

The result

The referral system drove half a million users over months of word-of-mouth. The design held for years, and the part I’m proudest of: it stayed stable, it didn’t break.

The problem

PayLah could only send money between people. To pay a real business, there had to be a merchant side that didn’t exist yet, and the first three businesses were nothing alike: a national payments network, a big payments company, and a tiny bus-ticket seller.

How would you build one way to pay a business that stays simple for the person paying, but still works for companies this different in size and setup?

What I did

Designed one reusable merchant capability rather than bespoke integrations: onboarding, a sandbox for partners to test against, the payment flow, settlement, and merchant notifications. Built the sandbox and onboarding so a partner could prove its integration safely before going live, and worked directly with each partner’s technical team to bring them on.

The result

The same flow carried three very different partners live: NETS (Singapore’s national payments standard, accepted in taxis), AXS (a high-volume payment aggregator), and BusOnlineTicket (a small bus-ticketing vendor). That range was the point. One product, built once, holding across the national standard, the large aggregator, and the small vendor, which is the real proof the architecture was right.

Conversational banking, DBS, 2016-2017

What I believe

A few things I'm convinced of.

  • When anyone can build, deciding what to build is the bottleneck.
  • Saying "I don't know the problem well enough" is where good product work begins.
  • The PRD didn't die. It became the artifact. The need for clarity never changed.
  • Context and structure beat a bigger model.
  • The best AI systems keep the human deciding, and do the rest well.
  • AI should make you sharper and more yourself, not dependent or generic.
