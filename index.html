The story

The hard part was never the building.

I started as an engineer who kept asking one question: why are we building this? It didn't always make me popular. But it turned out to be the only question that mattered.

At DBS in Singapore, that question became PayLah!, a payments app that grew to half a million downloads. At Ant Financial, working on Paytm, it meant cutting churn by a third by fixing what actually made people leave. At Gojek, I led product and design for Mapan, the financial-inclusion arm of a $10 billion super-app. In eighteen months we tripled revenue. Not by building more, but by being honest about what was worth building.

Knowing what deserves to exist is harder than building it. That gap is where I've spent my career.

In 2020, I tried to put that belief into a product. I called it MeNext. An AI that would interview you so you could write your own resume, in your own words, and own every word of it. Not a service that writes it for you. A tool that helps you think.

I never built it.

But I couldn't let go of the idea underneath it. AI should help you think and own your work, not do the thinking for you.

Then things changed. AI made building almost free. Anyone can ship now. And when everyone can build, the hard part becomes knowing what's worth building. The bottleneck moved, from making things to making the right call.

So I stopped waiting and started building again. The same idea I sketched in 2020, finally made real.

I started as an engineer who kept asking one question: why are we building this? It didn't always make me popular. But it turned out to be the only question that mattered.

At DBS in Singapore, I joined a small payments team and that question became PayLah!. We weren't just building a wallet. We were building the habit of paying differently. That distinction changed every decision: how we did onboarding, what the referral mechanic looked like, why we built a merchant API at all. The app grew to half a million downloads. I went from two to twelve on the team in six months. And in 2016, I shipped one of the first AI chatbots in banking, before anyone was calling it AI.

Then Ant Financial came calling. I joined to work on Paytm in India. The user base was enormous. The churn was too. We cut churn by a third. Not by launching features. By doing the analysis to understand exactly what made people leave, and then removing those reasons one by one.

Gojek was different. I joined to build a social app for Mapan, the unit serving women resellers across smaller-city Indonesia who earn a living selling to their neighbours. The mission was why I came. Through a reorg I became head of product and design, hired a new team, and reshaped how product, design, research, and engineering worked together. In eighteen months we tripled revenue and shipped the full social product.

DBS Singapore · 2014–2017

PayLah! — Singapore's first viral payments app

I joined a two-person PM team and built the referral mechanic that drove PayLah! to 500K downloads. My specific work: the onboarding flow, the peer-to-peer payment UX, the merchant API that let 3rd-party apps accept PayLah! payments, and a conversational AI chatbot for transaction queries — one of the first deployed in banking. I went from a team of 2 to leading a team of 12 within six months.

Ant Financial (Paytm) · 2018–2019

Cutting churn by a third through behaviour analysis

Paytm had scale but a churn problem. My job was to find out why users were leaving. I did a deep segmentation and behaviour cluster analysis — not surveys, actual usage patterns. We identified the specific failure moments that drove drop-off, then rebuilt those flows. Result: churn down by a third, monthly engagement up 15%. The lesson: most product problems are diagnostic problems in disguise.

Gojek / Mapan · 2019–2023

Building a social app for 130-person financial-inclusion arm

Mapan served women resellers across smaller-city Indonesia — people earning a living selling to their neighbours. I joined as lead PM to build the social product. Through a reorg I became Head of Product and Design: 5 PMs, 5 designers, embedded in a 130-person arm of a $10B super-app. I restructured how product, design, research, and engineering worked together. In 18 months we tripled revenue and shipped the full social app: feed, connections, leaderboard, and ranking system.

What I believe

A few things I'm convinced of.

  • When anyone can build, deciding what to build is the bottleneck.
  • AI should make you sharper and more yourself, not dependent or generic.
  • Most bad output is really a bad starting point. Context prepared up front beats endless prompting.
  • Clarity at the source compounds. Vagueness multiplies everything downstream.
  • Thinking gets better with the right questions in front of you. You shouldn't have to do it alone.
